{% elif prediction == 'basal cell carcinoma' %}

Basal cell carcinoma (BCC) is the most common type of skin cancer. It typically arises from the basal cells, which are found in the lowest layer of the epidermis, the outermost layer of the skin. Basal cell carcinoma usually develops on areas of the skin that have been exposed to the sun, such as the face, head, neck, and upper torso.

Basal cell carcinoma often appears as a raised, pink or flesh-colored, translucent bump on the skin. It may also present as a shiny or pearly nodule, a non-healing sore, or a scar-like area. BCCs can sometimes ulcerate or bleed, and they may have visible blood vessels or a crusty surface.

{% elif prediction == 'actinic keratosis' %}

Actinic keratosis (AK), also known as solar keratosis, is a rough, scaly patch on the skin that develops from years of exposure to the sun's ultraviolet (UV) rays. It is considered a precancerous condition because if left untreated, it can develop into skin cancer, most commonly squamous cell carcinoma.

Actinic keratoses typically appear on areas of the skin that are frequently exposed to the sun, such as the face, ears, neck, scalp, chest, backs of hands, and forearms. They often start as small, rough, red or pink patches that may feel dry or rough like sandpaper. Over time, they can become raised, thicker, and more noticeable.

{% elif prediction == 'dermatofibroma' %}

Dermatofibroma, also known as benign fibrous histiocytoma, is a common benign skin growth. It typically presents as a firm, raised nodule on the skin that is often brownish or reddish in color. Dermatofibromas usually develop on the legs, although they can occur on any part of the body.

These skin growths are thought to arise from fibroblasts, which are cells that produce connective tissue in the skin. The exact cause of dermatofibromas is not well understood, but they often occur in response to minor trauma or injury to the skin, such as insect bites or scratches.

{% elif prediction == 'keratosis' %}

"Keratosis" is a general term used to describe a group of skin conditions characterized by the thickening of the outer layer of the skin (epidermis). These conditions typically result in the formation of rough, scaly patches or growths on the skin.

Treatment for keratosis depends on the specific type and severity of the condition. It may include topical medications, cryotherapy (freezing), chemical peels, laser therapy, or surgical removal. It's important to consult a dermatologist for an accurate diagnosis and appropriate treatment plan for any skin condition.

{% elif prediction == 'melanoma' %}

Melanoma is a type of skin cancer that develops from the pigment-producing cells called melanocytes. Melanocytes are responsible for giving color to the skin and are found in the epidermis, the outermost layer of the skin. Melanoma can also occur in other parts of the body where melanocytes are present, such as the eyes, mucous membranes, and internal organs, although skin melanoma is the most common type.

Melanoma typically arises from abnormal or uncontrolled growth of melanocytes, often triggered by exposure to ultraviolet (UV) radiation from sunlight or tanning beds. However, it can also develop in areas of the skin that are not regularly exposed to the sun. Melanoma is often characterized by changes in the size, shape, color, or texture of existing moles, or the appearance of new pigmented lesions on the skin.

{% elif prediction == 'nevus' %}

A nevus, commonly known as a mole, is a benign (non-cancerous) growth on the skin. Moles are composed of melanocytes, the pigment-producing cells of the skin, and they can appear anywhere on the body. Most moles are harmless and do not require treatment, but in some cases, they may be surgically removed if they are causing symptoms or if there are concerns about their appearance.

While most moles are harmless, it's important to monitor them for changes in size, shape, color, or texture, as these changes could indicate melanoma or another type of skin cancer. If you notice any suspicious changes in a mole, such as asymmetry, irregular borders, uneven coloration, or evolving characteristics, it's essential to consult a dermatologist for evaluation and appropriate management. Additionally, regular skin examinations by a healthcare professional are recommended, especially for individuals with a history of significant sun exposure or a personal or family history of skin cancer.

{% elif prediction == 'squamous cell carcinoma' %}

Squamous cell carcinoma (SCC) is a common type of skin cancer that arises from the squamous cells, which are flat, thin cells that make up the outermost layer of the skin (epidermis). SCC typically develops on areas of the skin that have been exposed to the sun, such as the face, ears, neck, scalp, arms, and hands. However, it can also occur on mucous membranes, such as the lips, mouth, genitals, and anus, as well as on scars or chronic wounds.

Squamous cell carcinoma usually appears as a firm, red nodule or a flat lesion with a scaly or crusty surface. It may grow quickly and can sometimes become tender or painful. SCC has the potential to spread (metastasize) to other parts of the body, especially if left untreated. While it is less aggressive than melanoma, SCC can still cause significant morbidity and mortality if it invades surrounding tissues or metastasizes to distant organs.

{% elif prediction == 'vascular skin' %}

Vascular skin refers to skin that has visible blood vessels or vascular structures near the surface. This can manifest in various ways, including redness, visible veins, and vascular lesions.

Treatment options for vascular skin conditions depend on the specific condition and its severity. They may include laser therapy, sclerotherapy (injection of a solution into the blood vessel to shrink it), topical medications, or surgical removal. It's important to consult a dermatologist or healthcare provider for an accurate diagnosis and appropriate treatment plan for any vascular skin concerns.
